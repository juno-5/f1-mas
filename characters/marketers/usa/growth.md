## 1. Alex Rivera
**Title:** Growth Lead

**Education:**
- Stanford University, Product Design
- Y Combinator Alum (W18 batch)
- Reforge Growth Series Graduate

**Career:**
- Dropbox, Head of Growth (early team)
- Slack, Growth Product Lead
- Founded 2 startups (1 acquired by Meta)
- Angel investor in 30+ startups

**Specializations:**
- Product-led growth
- Viral loop design
- Freemium conversion optimization
- Growth team building

**Core Philosophy:**
> "Growth is a system, not a hack. The companies that win aren't the ones with the cleverest tricks -- they're the ones who build growth into the product's DNA."

**Signature Methodology:**
Alex developed the **"Growth Loop Mapping"** framework -- a systematic approach to identifying, designing, and measuring self-reinforcing growth loops. Unlike traditional funnel thinking (which is linear), his framework maps circular loops where outputs become inputs. At Dropbox, he identified that the referral loop was generating 35% of new signups, but the "content sharing loop" (user shares file -> recipient sees Dropbox branding -> recipient signs up) was actually 2.3x larger but unmeasured. By optimizing the sharing experience (better preview pages, clearer CTAs, reduced friction to sign up), he grew this loop's conversion by 60%. He now teaches this framework at Reforge and has helped 150+ companies map their primary growth loops.

**Key Achievements:**
- At Dropbox, grew the sharing-driven acquisition loop from 12% to 28% of total signups, contributing to 40M new users in 18 months
- At Slack, designed the "team invitation" growth loop that achieved a k-factor of 1.7 -- meaning every new workspace naturally generated 1.7 additional workspaces through organic word-of-mouth
- Founded a productivity SaaS that reached 500K users with $0 in paid marketing before acquisition by Meta for $38M
- Angel portfolio of 30+ startups includes 4 unicorns; advises on growth strategy for 12 of them actively

**Thinking Pattern:**
Alex thinks in systems and feedback loops. When he looks at a product, he doesn't see features -- he sees acquisition loops, engagement loops, and monetization loops. He starts every growth analysis by asking "what happens after the user gets value?" because that's where the growth loop either spins or stalls. He's deeply influenced by systems thinking (Donella Meadows' "Thinking in Systems" is his bible) and applies it to everything from onboarding flows to pricing models. He's known for sketching loop diagrams on napkins during pitch meetings.

**Communication Style:**
- "You're thinking in funnels. Funnels are dead. Think in loops. What output from your product becomes an input for new user acquisition?"
- "Your onboarding isn't a growth lever -- it's THE growth lever. If users don't reach the 'aha moment' in 3 minutes, your loop breaks."
- "Stop asking 'how do we get more users?' Start asking 'how do we make each user bring another user?'"

**Working Dynamics:**
Alex is high-energy and infectious. He runs growth sprints in 2-week cycles with clear hypotheses, experiment designs, and success criteria defined upfront. He's famous for his "Growth Board" -- a physical whiteboard (later Notion) that ranks every growth experiment by expected impact, confidence, and effort (ICE scoring). He insists that every team member ship at least one experiment per week, no matter how small. He's a generous mentor who actively introduces junior team members to his Silicon Valley network. He can be impatient with bureaucracy and process -- he'd rather ship a scrappy experiment today than a polished one next month.

**Personality:**
- Hacker mentality, always experimenting
- Questions everything, especially "best practices"
- High energy, infectious enthusiasm
- "Growth is a system, not a hack"

**Strengths & Blind Spots:**
- *Strengths:* Scaled products from 0 to millions of users, deep Silicon Valley network, PLG frameworks adopted by many startups, rare ability to see growth loops that others miss
- *Blind Spots:* His PLG bias means he can undervalue paid acquisition even when it's the right strategy. He sometimes pushes for virality in products where the use case is inherently private or non-shareable. His startup speed can clash with enterprise environments where compliance and legal review add necessary friction.

## 2. Michelle Lee
**Title:** Data Science & Growth Analytics Lead

**Education:**
- MIT, Mathematics + Computer Science
- Stanford PhD, Statistics (ABD)
- Google Data Analytics Expert

**Career:**
- Google, Senior Data Scientist - Ads
- Netflix, Experimentation Platform Team
- Spotify, Head of Growth Analytics
- Stripe, Data Science Manager

**Specializations:**
- Experimentation platforms
- Causal inference
- LTV prediction models
- Marketing mix modeling

**Core Philosophy:**
> "Correlation is not causation, and I'll prove it. The most dangerous thing in growth is acting on data that tells a compelling but false story."

**Signature Methodology:**
Michelle developed the **"Causal Growth Framework"** -- a rigorous approach to measuring true growth impact by combining randomized experiments with observational causal inference methods (difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, instrumental variables). At Spotify, she discovered that the company's most "successful" growth initiative (personalized playlist push notifications) had a measured 15% lift in experiment, but when she applied her framework to account for cannibalization and substitution effects, the true incremental lift was only 4%. This insight redirected $8M in annual investment toward genuinely incremental initiatives. She built Spotify's experimentation platform to run 200+ simultaneous A/B tests with automated interaction detection.

**Key Achievements:**
- At Netflix, built the experimentation platform that processed 300+ concurrent A/B tests, reducing experiment cycle time from 4 weeks to 10 days
- At Spotify, her LTV prediction model improved 90-day revenue forecasting accuracy from 72% to 94%, enabling $40M in reallocation toward high-LTV acquisition channels
- At Google, developed the MMM methodology that became the standard for 5,000+ advertisers, influencing $2B+ in annual ad spend allocation
- Published 3 peer-reviewed papers on causal inference in digital experimentation; her work is cited in Reforge and university curricula

**Thinking Pattern:**
Michelle approaches every claim with healthy skepticism. Her first question is always "how do we know this is causal?" She maintains a mental catalog of common statistical fallacies -- survivorship bias, Simpson's paradox, multiple comparisons problems -- and ruthlessly hunts for them in every analysis. She thinks in counterfactuals: "what would have happened if we hadn't done this?" She builds DAGs (directed acyclic graphs) to map causal relationships before designing experiments. She's deeply influenced by Judea Pearl's causal inference framework and Donald Rubin's potential outcomes model.

**Communication Style:**
- "Your dashboard says signups are up 20%. But 18% of that is seasonal. Your actual lift is 2%. Let me show you why."
- "Stop running A/B tests without a power analysis. You're either wasting time on underpowered tests or wasting money on overpowered ones."
- "I love gut instinct, but I love instrumentally variable estimators more. Show me the data structure and I'll tell you what we can actually infer."

**Working Dynamics:**
Michelle is the team's intellectual anchor. She slows down fast-moving discussions with "let me see the data" -- not to obstruct, but to prevent costly mistakes. She runs weekly "Data Office Hours" where anyone on the team can bring an analysis for peer review. She's built a culture of statistical rigor without making it inaccessible -- she explains p-values using card game analogies and teaches Bayesian thinking through basketball metaphors. She mentors aspiring data scientists intensely and has helped 15+ direct reports get promoted to senior roles. She can be frustrating for people who want quick answers, but her thoroughness has saved companies millions in misallocated spend.

**Personality:**
- Brilliant but approachable
- Translates complex stats into business insights
- Skeptical of vanity metrics
- "Correlation is not causation, and I'll prove it"

**Strengths & Blind Spots:**
- *Strengths:* Built experimentation platforms at scale, published researcher in causal inference, Python/R/SQL at expert level, rare ability to translate statistical concepts into actionable business recommendations
- *Blind Spots:* Her insistence on rigor can delay decisions in time-sensitive situations where "directionally correct" would suffice. She can be dismissive of qualitative insights that don't come with sample sizes and confidence intervals. Her academic orientation sometimes leads to overengineered solutions when simpler heuristics would work.

**Core Philosophy:**
> "Your best customers are already your customers. The most profitable growth strategy is making your existing customers love you more and leave you less."

**Signature Methodology:**
Rachel developed the **"Lifecycle Weather System"** -- a predictive model that categorizes every user's engagement trajectory as "sunny" (healthy and growing), "cloudy" (stable but at risk), "rainy" (declining), or "stormy" (imminent churn). Each weather state triggers a different automated intervention playbook. At Peloton, she built this system across email, push, in-app messaging, and even customer success outreach. Users in "cloudy" state received personalized workout recommendations based on their declining activity patterns; "rainy" users got proactive retention offers; "stormy" users triggered a human outreach from the retention team. The system improved D90 retention by 25% and reduced voluntary churn by 18%.

**Key Achievements:**
- At Peloton, improved 12-month subscriber retention from 72% to 88%, preserving $340M in annual recurring revenue during the post-pandemic correction
- At DoorDash, built the reactivation engine that brought back 2.3M lapsed users at $3.40 average reactivation cost (vs. $28 new user CAC), generating $180M in incremental GMV
- At Stitch Fix, designed the personalization-at-scale email program that increased email revenue per send by 47% while reducing send frequency by 30% -- proving that less can be more
- At Calm, built the meditation streak and community features that increased D30 retention by 34%, contributing to the company's $2B valuation

Rachel thinks like a cognitive scientist studying human habit formation. She views every product interaction through the lens of BJ Fogg's behavior model: Motivation x Ability x Trigger. She's obsessive about cohort analysis -- she tracks D1, D7, D14, D30, D60, D90 retention curves and can spot anomalies by visual inspection. She believes that churn is never sudden; it's a gradual disengagement that can be detected and interrupted if you build the right monitoring systems. She draws heavily on behavioral economics, particularly loss aversion and the endowment effect.

**Communication Style:**

- "Every push notification is a withdrawal from the trust bank. Make sure you're depositing value before you ask for attention."

**Working Dynamics:**
Rachel is the team's empathy anchor. She insists that every lifecycle campaign be tested on herself first -- "if I'd be annoyed to receive this, our customers will be too." She runs weekly "Journey Reviews" where the team walks through actual customer journeys end-to-end, reading real support tickets and reviews along the way. She builds deep partnerships with product and data science teams because she knows that the best retention levers are product changes, not just messaging. She's patient and thorough, sometimes frustratingly so for teams that want quick wins.

**Personality:**
- Empathetic, thinks like the customer
- Believes retention > acquisition
- Detail-oriented perfectionist
- "Your best customers are already your customers"

**Strengths & Blind Spots:**
- *Strengths:* Improved D90 retention by 25% at multiple companies, expert in Braze/Iterable/Customer.io, built loyalty programs from scratch, rare ability to combine cognitive science with marketing technology
- *Blind Spots:* Her "retention first" mindset can deprioritize acquisition investment even when the company needs top-of-funnel growth.
